# GuideWire-Deployers
## Inspiration

India has over 15 lakh active food delivery partners working for Swiggy and Zomato. When a flood hits Mumbai, a curfew is declared in Delhi, or an LPG shortage shuts down a city zone, these riders earn ₹0 — but their rent, EMIs, and family expenses don't stop. Traditional insurance doesn't help here because it needs forms, documents, and weeks of waiting. By the time a claim gets processed, the crisis is already over and the financial damage is done.
We asked one question: what if the insurance system detected the disaster itself and sent money to every affected rider's UPI before they even opened the app? That question became DelivrShield. The idea came from looking at the real numbers — the average Zomato delivery partner is only active 38 days a year. Most riders are part-time or seasonal. They can't afford a ₹500/month fixed premium and they shouldn't have to. A product that charges ₹20/week based on how much you actually work, adjusts automatically, and gets deducted invisibly — that's the only kind of insurance a gig worker will actually stick with long term.

## What it does

DelivrShield is a usage-based income protection system for food delivery partners. It works entirely without the rider doing anything after enrolling once. Riders contribute a small amount per delivery — ₹1.00 in Tier 1 metros, ₹0.75 in Tier 2 cities, ₹0.50 in Tier 3 towns — automatically deducted from their existing weekly Swiggy/Zomato payout. A full-time Mumbai rider doing 100 deliveries a week contributes ₹100/week, which is about 1.5% of weekly earnings and completely invisible. A part-time Tier 3 rider doing 40 deliveries a week pays just ₹20/week — less than the cost of a meal.
When a qualifying event happens — curfew, flood, cyclone, bandh, platform outage over 6 hours, or a weather red alert — our AI detects it automatically using news feeds, government alert systems, weather APIs, and platform order volume data. It then checks each affected rider's GPS location, confirms their orders have dropped to zero, runs a fraud check, and sends a daily UPI payment with no form filing needed from the rider. Payouts are ₹250–₹500/day for Tier 3 riders and ₹400–₹900/day for Tier 1 riders, covering 65–75% of estimated daily earnings for up to 14 days per event. The weekly contribution isn't fixed either — it scales with how many deliveries the rider actually completes that week. If the whole zone is down because of an external cause like an LPG shortage, the AI detects that too and protects the rider's coverage without penalising their lower delivery count. Riders who complete 6 months without a claim get a 15% reduction in their per-delivery rate for the next period, plus a higher payout ceiling — no cash is returned from the pool, keeping the system financially sustainable while giving loyal riders real ongoing value.


## How we built it
The system is built across five AI modules, each with a different technical approach chosen based on what actually fits the problem best.
Module 1 — Disruption Detection uses a TF-IDF vectoriser with a Logistic Regression classifier to read real-time news text from NewsAPI, GDELT, government RSS feeds like PIB and NDMA, and IMD weather alerts. spaCy NER pulls out the affected pin codes and place names from alert text. We chose TF-IDF and Logistic Regression over BERT on purpose — government alerts and news headlines are short, keyword-driven text where phrases like "Section 144 imposed" or "red alert flood" are the actual signal. TF-IDF catches these exactly, runs in milliseconds, and every word that triggered the alert is fully visible and explainable — which matters a lot for insurance regulation. An alert only fires when at least 2 independent sources agree, so single rumours or misreported stories don't cause false payouts.
Module 2 — Zone Anomaly Engine uses NumPy and Pandas to compute a Zone Delivery Index every week by comparing actual zone-wide delivery volume against an 8-week rolling average. A Z-score flags statistical drops, and then Isolation Forest runs on a richer set of features — delivery count, active rider count, average order value, weather severity — to confirm whether the drop has an identifiable external cause. If the cause is confirmed with over 70% confidence, affected riders' coverage is maintained at their 4-week average so they aren't penalised for a zone-wide collapse that wasn't their fault.
Module 3 — Tier Freeze Engine runs the moment a disruption alert fires. It snapshots every affected rider's 4-week average weekly contribution and locks their coverage tier for the entire event. This makes sure a rider's payout isn't calculated on reduced deliveries they did during the disaster itself.
Module 4 — Fraud Detection uses an XGBoost classifier that scores each claim across GPS spoofing signals like accelerometer-GPS mismatch, cell tower distance contradiction, and coordinate jitter smoothness, plus behavioural signals like delivery history consistency and claim frequency, plus cluster signals that catch groups of riders spoofing together with matching device profiles. We chose XGBoost over neural networks because fraud signals are structured and tabular, and XGBoost's SHAP explanations let us show exactly why a claim was rejected — to the rider, to an auditor, or to a regulator.
Module 5 — Eligibility and Payout Engine is a straightforward rule-based system, not an ML model. It checks policy status, minimum contribution weeks (3), GPS zone match, order count, and fraud score, then runs the payout formula. Insurance payout decisions have to be fully auditable and explainable — a rejected rider must get a clear reason, not a probability score from a black box.
The backend is FastAPI on Python. PostgreSQL with PostGIS handles zone-level geospatial queries. Celery and Redis run the weekly pricing batch jobs and the news polling loops. Everything runs in Docker containers on Google Cloud. The rider app and operations dashboard are built in React with Tailwind CSS and Chart.js.


## Challenges we ran into
Building an insurance system around weekly micro-payments was genuinely hard. Traditional insurance is designed for fixed monthly premiums from salaried employees — gig workers don't work like that. A rider might do 100 deliveries one week and 20 the next, or take two weeks off entirely. We couldn't charge a fixed amount because that's unfair in low-activity weeks, and we couldn't skip weeks because that creates exploitable coverage gaps. The formula we landed on ties the contribution directly to actual delivery activity: weekly premium = (rider's deliveries that week ÷ zone's 8-week average) × tier base rate × actual deliveries. A slow week means a smaller contribution automatically. Coverage only suspends if a rider does zero deliveries for a full week, not just a slow one. Getting that balance — fair without being exploitable — took more back and forth than anything else in the model.

Designing the premium and payout calculation system so it felt fair at every income level took a lot of careful work. The per-delivery rate varies by city tier — ₹1.00 in Tier 1 metros, ₹0.75 in Tier 2, ₹0.50 in Tier 3 towns — so that a full-time rider in any tier pays roughly 1.3–1.5% of their weekly earnings regardless of where they live. When a disaster triggers a payout, the daily amount is calculated from the rider's last 4 weeks of contribution history: we back-calculate their estimated daily earnings from how much they contributed, then pay out 65–75% of that figure depending on tier, with hard floors (minimum ₹250/day for Tier 3, ₹400/day for Tier 1) and ceilings (maximum ₹500/day for Tier 3, ₹900/day for Tier 1). The floor makes sure even a part-time rider gets a meaningful amount. The ceiling makes sure the payout is always less than normal earnings, so there's no reason for anyone to hope for a disaster. Payouts run for up to 14 days per event, after which the rider is expected to return to work
## Accomplishments that we're proud of
The complete zero-human-intervention pipeline is the core thing we're proud of — disruption detected, riders verified, fraud scored, payout triggered, all without a single claim form, a single call centre agent, or a single day of processing delay. For workers who have historically been locked out of formal financial protection, getting money to a rider's UPI within hours of a disaster is the whole point of the product.
The pricing model's proportional fairness across income levels is something we worked hard to get right. A Tier 3 rider earning ₹1,600/week and a Tier 1 rider earning ₹6,500/week both pay roughly 1.3–1.5% of their weekly earnings. The premium never feels heavier as a share of income regardless of where you live or how much you earn. That required careful calibration of the per-tier base rates against real delivery volume and earnings data rather than picking round numbers.
The fraud detection design works at the hardware level — it exploits physical constraints on what a software mock location app can and cannot fake. That came from first principles thinking about the problem rather than copying existing fraud detection patterns, and it produces a fraud score that is both effective and explainable without needing any custom hardware on the rider's device.
The unit economics are real and solid. 54.8% net margin on Tier 1 riders, 47.9% on Tier 3, with a projected ₹13.8 Cr annual net profit at 1 lakh enrolled riders across tiers. The no-claim loyalty discount is self-funding — no-claim riders have around 75% margin in their claim-free period, so even after the 15% rate reduction their next period still runs above 50% margin.

##Adversarial Defense & Anti-Spoofing Strategy

1. The Differentiation — Genuine Rider vs Location Spoofer
A GPS spoofing app lives entirely in software, but a real phone exists in the physical world — and these two things leave completely different traces. A spoofing app injects fake coordinates into the Android OS location API, but it cannot touch the accelerometer (a separate hardware chip), cannot change what the magnetometer reads (it measures Earth's actual magnetic field at the real location), and cannot alter the radio physics between the phone and its nearest cell tower. It also produces unnaturally smooth GPS coordinates, because it generates them mathematically rather than receiving them from real satellites, which always produce slight natural noise.
A genuinely stranded rider passes all physical signal checks automatically. A spoofer fails on multiple of them simultaneously — and that combination is what triggers a fraud flag.

2. The Data — Beyond GPS Coordinates
We analyse three layers of signals. The first is hardware-level physical signals — accelerometer vs GPS movement mismatch, magnetometer reading vs expected Earth field at claimed coordinates, cell tower distance vs claimed location, and GPS coordinate jitter (real GPS is noisy; spoofed GPS is suspiciously smooth). The second is cross-signal verification — IP geolocation vs claimed GPS, Play Integrity API check for rooted devices or mock location enabled, and satellite metadata consistency. The third is behavioural and cluster signals — this is the most important layer for catching coordinated group fraud. We look at how many riders are claiming simultaneously from the same area, whether their device fingerprints are unusually similar, whether their submission timing is unnaturally synchronised, and whether their 30-day delivery history actually matches the zone they're claiming from. A real mass disruption produces claims from hundreds of varied devices with natural timing. A fraud ring produces a small cluster of similar devices with near-identical submission times and delivery histories that often don't match the claimed zone at all.

3. The UX Balance — Flagged Claims Without Punishing Honest Riders
The system has three response tiers. Score 0–30 means auto-approved — payout hits UPI within minutes, no human involved. Score 31–60 means a silent 24-hour soft hold — the rider is simply told their payout is processing, a secondary automated check runs quietly in the background, and if nothing new emerges the payout releases automatically. Most genuine riders in this band never notice the delay. Score 61–100 means a human review within 48 hours — the ops agent sees a full SHAP breakdown of exactly which signals triggered the flag and why, so they can take a targeted action like a quick verification call rather than blindly rejecting the claim.
Any rejected rider gets a specific written reason and a clear appeal path — not a generic denial. Appeals are reviewed by a human within 72 hours. The system is deliberately calibrated to be aggressive on coordinated group fraud where signals are strong, and cautious at the individual level where evidence is ambiguous — because falsely rejecting one genuine claim during a flood does more damage to rider trust than paying out one claim that slipped through.


## What we learned

Parametric insurance — where the payout trigger is an objective external event rather than a filed claim — is not new, but applying it to gig economy income protection at micro-contribution scale meant rethinking almost every standard insurance design assumption. The biggest shift is that the product has to work for someone who is only active 38 days a year, not a full-time employee with a steady salary to deduct premiums from.
We learned that simpler models are often the right technical choice, not a compromise. TF-IDF and Logistic Regression outperform BERT for short government alert text on latency, interpretability, and practical deployability. In insurance specifically, interpretability is a hard regulatory requirement — not a nice-to-have — so choosing a more complex model that performs marginally better but can't explain its decisions would actually be the wrong call. Asking "is this model actually better for this specific problem, or does it just sound more impressive" shaped every model decision in the system.
We also learned that the emotional and psychological design of the product is just as important as the technical design. A rider paying ₹520 over 6 months who never has to claim doesn't need a cash refund — they need to genuinely feel that their money meant something. The framing of the no-claim benefit as "you protected yourself against a risk that didn't happen this time, and now we're rewarding your consistency" was a more consequential design decision than any hyperparameter choice in any of the five modules.

## What's next for DelivrShield

The immediate next step is a pilot with 500–1,000 riders in a single Tier 1 city, integrated directly into one platform's existing weekly payout settlement cycle. This gives us real claims data to validate our disruption event rate assumptions, check the fraud detection thresholds under real conditions, and see whether the no-claim loyalty benefit actually drives the retention behaviour we expect.
Beyond food delivery, the same model architecture applies to any gig economy vertical with verifiable activity signals and discrete disruption events — auto-rickshaw drivers with GPS and booking platform data, construction day labourers with site check-in signals, domestic workers using attendance apps. The Zone Delivery Index concept generalises to any platform that tracks worker activity at a zone level. On the regulatory side, we are mapping DelivrShield to IRDAI's sandbox framework for micro-insurance products, which allows pilot operations without a full insurance licence while the actuarial track record is being built. The long-run vision is a gig worker protection layer that any platform can embed into their payout cycle with a single API integration — making income protection something that is simply part of how platforms pay their workers, not a separate product workers have to go find and buy on their own.
